Most physical-product companies still plan inventory off an annual budget set
once, in a spreadsheet, in Q4. By February it's wrong. By June it's fiction.

A **rolling 12-month forecast** fixes the structural problem: instead of one
fixed horizon that decays all year, it always looks twelve months ahead and
re-bases on the latest actuals every month.

## What "rolling" actually changes

- **Your horizon never shrinks.** In December, an annual plan has one month of
  visibility left. A rolling forecast still sees a full year.
- **Actuals feed back in.** Each month's real sales, returns, and lead-time
  slips update the model — so next month's order recommendations reflect what
  happened, not what you assumed in January.
- **Purchase orders adjust continuously.** Instead of an annual re-plan
  scramble, POs nudge in small, defensible increments.

## Where it pays off

The compounding win is in **working capital**. When the forecast tightens, your
target days-on-hand can come down without raising stockout risk — releasing cash
that was sitting in slow-moving inventory.

> Rule of thumb: every day of days-on-hand you remove from a well-classified
> catalog frees roughly one day of COGS in cash.
